Algal Research Projects: John Kinross

1 Indicators: the concept of biological indicators. (see the US EPA's site on periphyton as indicators)

Biological communities are composed of species each of which has its own preferred conditions for survival. Therefore the makeup of a community (the proportions of the various species = community structure) is a reflection of the environmental conditions at a particular time and place: the community structure is an ecological indicator of those conditions.

This relationship has been widely used in the monitoring of streamwater pollution, using macroinvertebrate communities. It has also been found that certain algal groups are useful indicators. Diatoms in particular leave behind their silica frustules (cell cases) as fossil remains, by which they can be identified in sediments, and have been useful in the reconstruction of pH changes in lakes. Chrysophytes also have silicaceous wall parts which are preserved in sediments and these have recently been found useful indicators of changes in pH, salinity, phosphorus and metal concentrations in lake waters.

Filamentous benthic algae leave no fossil remains but their community structure is a good indicator of low-pH conditions in which few diatom species survive (Kinross et al, 1993).

Project 1: Remaining questions of interest are: how does the (normal) longitudinal gradient in pH within a single stream (pH lowest near the source) influence the community structure, and is this independent of the parallel change in stream size?

Is the relationship between filamentous algal community structure and streamwater chemistry due to direct pH preferences, or a secondary effect due to elimination of invertebrate grazers, for example? A test of growth rates of various species at different pH's showed that those most commonly found in low-pH streams had a growth rate optimum at a lower pH than those more common at higher pH (Kinross et al, 2000.). 2 Molecular ecological studies

Identification of many algae to species level is problematic due to phenotypic plasticity (the tendency to different growth forms in response to different environmental conditions), and the necessity to observe the products of sexual reproduction in some other genera.
Project 3: A possible resolution to this problem may come from molecular studies such as 'genetic fingerprinting'. This approach may also shed light on the degree of relatedness of algal populations from adjacent watersheds - of interest as it would indicate how isolated such populations are, and whether genetic drift and species differentiation could occur (since some filamentous green algae appear not to have a sexual means of reproduction, these should show greater differences than sexually reproducing species).

3 Use of algae in wastewater treatment

Algal mats are able to strip nutrients from solution. This could form the basis of biological tertiary treatment of wastewaters.
Project 4: A pilot study is under way into the nutrient stripping potential of green algal mats and Duckweed (Lemna) -see photograph.

4 Autecology of algae

Species within a single are by definition closely related and will have similar environmental requirements (similar niches), so how did they evolve into different species at all? Are there detectable differences between species' environmental preferences?

Many green algal species occur in the same (apparent) habitat as others of the same genus (eg Mougeotia, Oedogonium), permitting tests of this hypothesis:
Project 5: Differences may exist in tolerance to high/low light levels, pH, temperature, response to nutrients (including micronutrients), desiccation, or susceptibility to grazing.
